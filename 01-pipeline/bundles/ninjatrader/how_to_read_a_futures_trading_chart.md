# How to Read a Futures Trading Chart

By NinjaTrader

August 12, 2025

Futures trading charts are more than just lines and candlesticks—they’re your gateway to understanding market behavior in real time. Whether you’re just starting your trading journey or looking to sharpen your skills, mastering how to read a futures chart can give you the edge you need to spot opportunities, time your entries, and make smarter, more confident decisions.

This guide will walk you through the key elements of futures charts, showing you how to decode price movement, uncover trends, and recognize trade setups with clarity and precision.


## What is a futures trading chart?

A futures trading chart is a visual representation of price action over time. It plots market data—such as price, volume, and time intervals—to help traders observe trends, identify patterns, and make decisions about when to enter or exit trades.

You can customize charts to fit your preferences, including time frames, indicators, and chart styles. No matter the setup, the goal remains the same: to simplify complex price data and uncover actionable insights to help you make better trading decisions.


## Common futures trading chart types

There are several ways to view price movement on a futures trading chart, and each style serves a purpose, depending on what you’re analyzing.

- Line chart: The simplest chart type; it connects closing prices over a set period with a continuous line and is ideal for quickly spotting general trends

- Bar chart: Offers more detail than a line chart; each bar displays the open, high, low, and close (OHLC) for a given time interval

- Candlestick chart: One of the most popular chart types among futures traders; like a bar chart, it displays OHLC data but in an intuitive way that can help identify trends and patterns

- Volume chart: Plots trading volume alongside price action to help traders gauge the strength behind a move. Learn how to analyze volume in the futures markets.


## Key components of a futures trading chart

Futures trading charts contain a range of data that, when interpreted correctly, can help you understand market dynamics.


### 1. Price axis (vertical)

Located on the right-hand side of the chart, the price axis shows the current price levels for the asset you’re tracking.


### 2. Time axis (horizontal)

Located along the bottom, this axis displays the time intervals selected (e.g., 5 minutes, hourly, daily), allowing you to view price movement over a chosen period.


### 3. Chart bars and candles

Each bar and candle represent a single period, depending on your selected time frame. These visual elements tell you how price behaved during that interval.

- Open: Where the price started

- High/low: The price range during the period

- Close: Where the price ended


### 4. Indicators and overlays

You can enhance your charts with technical indicators, which can help you interpret price momentum, trend strength, and potential reversal points. Common indicators include:

- Moving average: Helps smooth price action and identify trends

- Relative strength index (RSI): Measures momentum and potential overbought/oversold conditions. Learn more about RSI.

- Volume indicator: Shows how much interest there is in a particular price move


## Understanding trends and patterns

Recognizing trends is one of the most fundamental uses of reading a futures trading chart. A trend reflects the general direction of price movement: upward (uptrend), downward (downtrend), or sideways (consolidation).

Chart patterns —such as triangles, head and shoulders, and double tops/bottoms—can signal potential continuation and reversal points. While patterns cannot guarantee outcome, they can help you assess probabilities and set strategic entry and exit levels.


## Choosing the right time frame

Futures charts are highly customizable, and your time frame selection will depend on your trading style.

- Short-term traders may prefer 1-minute to 15-minute charts for quick decisions.

- Swing traders might use hourly or daily charts to track price over several days.

- Long-term traders often rely on daily, weekly, or monthly charts for a broader perspective.

The time frame you choose will influence how much noise or clarity you’ll see in the chart data. It’s common for traders to reference multiple time frames to gain a more complete picture.


## Putting it all together

By learning how to read a futures trading chart, you can better understand market context, spot trade setups, and manage risk. It’s not about predicting the markets—it's about reading what the chart is telling you in real time.
